Women’s Economic Opportunities

Women now comprise more than half of the U.S. workforce, earn half of college degrees and hold half of management and professional jobs. Yet relatively few have gained top executive and political leadership positions, and women still earn less than men for comparable work. Moreover, flexible work arrangements, paid family leave and other accommodations designed to relieve domestic pressures shouldered largely by women remain elusive.

Employment and Earnings

Women make up about half of the workforce (47% in 2012) but on average still only make 77% of what their male counterparts do yearly, a number that has changed very little in the last 14 years. This number dips even lower for women of minority groups; African American women earn 66%, and Latino women 56% of what their white male counterparts do. The issue is not that women are less educated than men: in 2012, 45% of women were enrolled in colleges, versus only 38% of men. However, women still deal with the gender wage gap in male dominated, gender balanced, and female dominated fields. Ideally, the next president will work towards closing the wage gap entirely. Although legislation is already in place, including the Equal Pay Act and others similar in nature, more must be done to ensure women are receiving proper education, opportunities, equal pay, and do not experience gender-based discrimination.

Paid Sick and Parental Leave

America is still the ONLY developed country in the world that does not guarantee paid leave for mothers. However, maternal leave remains a necessity for new mothers: in 2013, about 70% of mothers of kids under the age of 18 were in the work force. The problem, however, has continued to worsen. As of 2014, 12% of companies offered paid maternity leave - a number that decreased from 17% in 2010. The only legislation presently in place is The Family and Medical Leave Act, which has much to be improved upon - only 12 weeks of UNPAID leave are required by this act. And even so, in 2014, only 19% of companies allow leave beyond these 12 weeks-- down from 26% in 2013. Unpaid leave is detrimental to family life in America, because most families cannot afford taking unpaid time off for the length of time their children need. Studies show that children of mothers who return to work up to 12 weeks of giving birth are more likely to be behind on immunizations and regular doctor checkups, and will not receive sufficient breastfeeding in the first year of life. In a country which stresses the importance of family, more must be done to ensure the health quality of life in American families. Currently, a handful of states have required paid leave. In these states, women are far less likely to use food stamps or public assistance - only 10% as compared with 25% in states without such legislation. The answer is clear - every working mother deserves and needs access to paid leave.
