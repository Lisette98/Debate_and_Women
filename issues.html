Why We Need a Women’s Debate

Below are three main areas that have been identified as important for discussion amongst our politicians. This is by no means an exhaustive list of issues, but serves as a framework to find our collective voice.


Healthcare


Access to quality affordable health care for all

Despite significant progress, the Affordable Healthcare Act (ACA) reforms remain unavailable to women for whom health insurance itself is out of reach. Additionally women are at higher risk of being uninsured, making access to healthcare an urgent issue. And even women who do have insurance, deal with higher prices and uncertainty of coverage.

Economic Opportunities


Eliminate barriers that inhibit equitable participation in the economy

Women now comprise more than half of the U.S. workforce, earn half of college degrees and hold half of management and professional jobs. Yet relatively few have gained top executive and political leadership positions, and women still earn less than men for comparable work. Moreover, flexible work arrangements, paid family leave and other accommodations designed to relieve domestic pressures shouldered largely by women remain elusive.

Women have suffered steep declines in take-home pay and employment, and a faster rise in poverty than men since the Great Recession, especially among single mothers of children under age 18 (39.8%).

Personal Safety


Safe and just recourse for women and girls when it comes to violence in the home, on campus, in the military and in prisons.

Violence against women in the United States occurs in many forms, including domestic violence, human trafficking, sexual assault on campus and in the military. More can be done to provide support for survivors, hold perpetrators accountable, challenge sexism that perpetuates violence, and show leadership on the international stage to end violence against women in all forms.
